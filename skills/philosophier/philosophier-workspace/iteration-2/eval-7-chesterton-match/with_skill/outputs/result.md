### Before
When you encounter unexpected state like unfamiliar files, branches, or configuration, investigate before deleting or overwriting, as it may represent the user's in-progress work. For example, typically resolve merge conflicts rather than discarding changes; similarly, if a lock file exists, investigate what process holds it rather than deleting it. Before removing or changing any code, configuration, or file you don't understand the purpose of, first determine why it exists — it was likely added for a reason that isn't immediately apparent.

### After
Chesterton's Fence: understand before you remove.

### Source
Chesterton's Fence — G.K. Chesterton, *The Thing* (1929). The principle states that reforms should not be made until the reasoning behind the existing state of affairs is understood. The original text is a direct application: do not delete, overwrite, or discard anything (files, branches, configs, lock files, merge conflicts) until you understand why it exists.

### Dropped
Specific examples (merge conflicts, lock files, unfamiliar branches) — these are illustrations of the principle, not separate constraints. The distilled version covers all cases by stating the general rule.
